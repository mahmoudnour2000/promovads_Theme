WHO Approves World's First mRNA Malaria Vaccine With 94% Efficacy — A Potential Game-Changer

The breakthrough vaccine, developed jointly by Moderna and the Gates Foundation, could prevent up to 400,000 deaths annually and eliminate malaria in high-burden regions within a decade.

Dr. Sarah Ahmed, MD
Senior Medical Correspondent · Reviewed by Editorial Board
June 15, 2026 6 min read 182,000 views

Key Facts About the Vaccine

  • 94% efficacy in Phase 3 trials across 12 African countries
  • Developed using mRNA technology — same platform as COVID-19 vaccines
  • Provides protection for 4+ years with a 3-dose schedule
  • Approved for children 6 months to 5 years of age
  • WHO recommends implementation in 43 high-burden malaria countries
  • Production capacity: 200M doses/year by end of 2026

A Historic Achievement

The World Health Organization's approval of RTS,S2 — the world's first mRNA-based malaria vaccine — represents one of the most significant achievements in global health history. After 60 years of attempting to develop an effective malaria vaccine, the combination of mRNA technology and revolutionary adjuvant development has delivered a solution that could transform the landscape of infectious disease prevention in the developing world.

Who Developed It?

The vaccine was developed through an unprecedented collaboration between Moderna's mRNA research unit, the Gates Foundation's malaria initiative, PATH, and the Serum Institute of India. The project received $4.2 billion in funding over 8 years.

"This is the vaccine that generations of scientists dreamed of. Malaria has killed more humans than any disease in history — today that changes." — Dr. Tedros Adhanom, WHO Director-General

Distribution Plan

WHO and GAVI have pre-purchased 400 million doses to be distributed free of charge to the 43 highest-burden countries. The rollout begins in Ghana, Kenya, and Nigeria in August 2026, with full deployment across sub-Saharan Africa by 2027.

The price per dose for high-income countries is $12, making it significantly more accessible than previous malaria prevention tools.
