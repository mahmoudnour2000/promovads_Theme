Federal Reserve Holds Rates Steady at 4.25% — Markets Rally on Positive Inflation Data

The central bank's decision aligns with consensus expectations as Core PCE falls to 2.1%, the closest to the Fed's 2% target since 2021. Markets interpret the statement as signaling a September cut.

The Federal Open Market Committee voted unanimously to keep the target range for the federal funds rate at 4.25%–4.50% at its June meeting, as widely expected. However, the accompanying statement contained notably more dovish language than March's meeting, prompting an immediate rally in equities and a drop in Treasury yields.

Chair Jerome Powell, speaking at the post-meeting press conference, acknowledged that "inflation has made meaningful progress" toward the 2% target, while noting that the labor market remains "broadly balanced." The key phrase markets latched onto was Powell's comment that "the question of when to begin reducing rates is now approaching."

Market Reaction

The S&P 500 jumped 0.94% by mid-afternoon, with rate-sensitive sectors leading gains. Technology stocks, which benefit from lower discount rates, outperformed with the Nasdaq rising 1.12%. The 10-year Treasury yield fell 8 basis points to 4.28%, its lowest in three weeks.

"This is the most dovish statement since before the hiking cycle began. A September cut is now the base case." — Mohamed El-Erian, Allianz Chief Economic Adviser

What's Next

Markets are now pricing in a 68% probability of a 25-basis-point cut at the September 16 meeting, according to CME FedWatch. Two cuts are now expected by year-end, up from one cut priced in before today's statement.

The next major data points that will influence the Fed's decision include July CPI (August 13), July PCE (August 30), and August jobs report (September 5).
